# Jobs Pipeline · Roles that pay for this skill set

**Today's workflow — passive corporate recon, dossier production,
source rating — is a skill ten different roles will pay you to use.
This reference is the menu.**

Pick one row. Start moving toward it this week.

---

## The roles

### Threat Intelligence Analyst
**Day-to-day:** Track threat actors, malware campaigns, brand threats,
geopolitical risk. Produce finished intel products for internal
stakeholders. Heavy reading, heavy writing, heavy attribution work.

**Hires:** F500 corporate security teams. Vendors like Mandiant,
Recorded Future, Flashpoint, Cybersixgill, ZeroFox. Government
contractors.

**What to study:** SANS FOR578 (Cyber Threat Intel). MITRE ATT&CK
framework. Diamond Model of Intrusion Analysis. Practice writing
intel products end-to-end. Read other people's published TI reports
critically.

---

### Red Team Operator (recon lead) / Pentester
**Day-to-day:** Pre-engagement external recon, asset discovery, initial
access prep. The recon phase makes or breaks engagements. Today's
workflow IS the first 30% of any red team operation.

**Hires:** Boutique consultancies (Bishop Fox, NCC Group, TrustedSec,
Black Hills Information Security). F500 internal red teams. Government
red teams.

**What to study:** OSCP → OSEP, or PNPT (TCM Security). HackTheBox,
TryHackMe. Recon-focused courses from TrustedSec. Build a portfolio
of dossiers on bug bounty targets to show the work.

---

### Third-Party Risk Analyst (TPRM)
**Day-to-day:** Evaluate vendor security before and during contracts.
OSINT fills the gap between what vendors self-report on questionnaires
and what's actually true.

**Hires:** F500 procurement and risk teams. Banks, insurance, healthcare
(regulated industries with heavy vendor exposure). GRC consultancies.

**What to study:** ISO 27001, SOC 2, NIST CSF. Shared Assessments SIG
methodology. Vendor security assessment frameworks.

---

### M&A Cyber Due Diligence
**Day-to-day:** Pre-acquisition cyber risk assessment. External OSINT
reveals what management didn't disclose during diligence. The OSINT
findings often re-price deals.

**Hires:** Big 4 (Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PwC). Boutique cyber advisory
(Aon/Stroz Friedberg, FTI Consulting). PE/VC firms with in-house cyber
teams.

**What to study:** CISSP, CISA. M&A process frameworks. Enough
accounting fundamentals to read SEC filings critically (today's
Module 1 is exactly this).

---

### Bug Bounty Hunter
**Day-to-day:** Find and responsibly disclose vulnerabilities for cash.
Recon is half the battle on any wide-scope program. Hunters who
out-recon other hunters win.

**Hires:** Self-employed. HackerOne Triage as a day job (full-time
employed by H1 to triage incoming reports). Some companies hire
in-house "bug bounty enablement" staff.

**What to study:** PortSwigger Web Security Academy (free, excellent).
TJ Null's OSCP-prep box list. Live-hunt any HackerOne, Bugcrowd, or
Intigriti scope to build experience and reputation.

---

### Brand Protection / Anti-Counterfeit
**Day-to-day:** Detect fraud, phishing, fake products, brand
impersonation, IP theft from the outside. Continuous monitoring of
the brand's external surface.

**Hires:** F500 in-house brand teams. Specialized firms like
MarkMonitor, Allure Security, Group-IB, ZeroFox.

**What to study:** Trademark fundamentals. DMCA process. Typosquatting
research. Phishing kit investigation. Domain monitoring at scale.

---

### Investigative Journalism / OSINT Research
**Day-to-day:** Long-form investigations using public sources. The
Bellingcat model. Same dossier-building skill, different end product
(article instead of internal report).

**Hires:** ProPublica, Bellingcat, NYT/WaPo investigative desks.
Regional nonprofits like Texas Tribune, ICIJ. Increasingly, independent
Substack-supported journalists.

**What to study:** Bellingcat's free online resources are world-class.
C4ADS reports for case studies. Stanford JSK Fellowship resources.
Maltego for visualization. Writing for non-technical audiences.

---

### Cyber-Enabled Private Investigation
**Day-to-day:** Asset tracing, skip tracing, divorce work, fraud
investigation, missing persons. Increasingly digital — modern PI work
is more keyboard than gumshoe.

**Hires:** Private investigation firms. Some law firms have in-house
investigators. Trace Labs offers volunteer experience that builds
into paid work.

**What to study:** Your state's PI licensing requirements (varies
wildly — some states require apprenticeship hours, some don't license
at all). Civil evidence and chain-of-custody. Michael Bazzell's books
and podcast.

---

### Pre-Sales Security Engineering
**Day-to-day:** Help your sales team qualify prospects by reconning
their actual security posture. Nobody else on the sales team can do
this. Recon-heavy SE work commands a premium.

**Hires:** Any security vendor — CrowdStrike, Palo Alto, SentinelOne,
Recorded Future, Censys, Mandiant, hundreds more.

**What to study:** Your prospective vendor's products and certs.
Sales methodology (MEDDIC, SPIN). General security foundations
strong enough to handle technical objections from prospects'
security teams.

---

### Solo Consulting LLC (the Wildcard Industries path)
**Day-to-day:** Package multiple services above for mid-market clients
who can't afford F500 prices. M&A diligence for a private-equity firm
buying a small manufacturer. TPRM for a regional bank. Brand monitoring
for a growing DTC company. You pick what fits your week.

**Hires:** Yourself. Your network. Referrals from people who heard you
present at events like this one.

**What to study:** All of the above, **plus** LLC formation in your
state. Contracts. Invoicing. Sales. Marketing. Pricing. Reading
proposed scopes critically. SCORE mentorship is free and excellent.
Paul Jarvis, *Company of One*. Not glamorous, mostly invisible work,
high autonomy.

## What hiring managers actually want — regardless of role

Five things. In this order.

1. **Documentation discipline.** Your dossiers prove this. The
   Admiralty-rated, source-cited, timestamped product you walk out
   with today is exactly what hiring managers ask portfolio
   candidates for. Save it. Reuse the format.

2. **Python (or equivalent scripting) literacy.** Not "I can write
   Django apps." More like "I can chain API calls, parse JSON,
   massage data, and produce a useful output." Today's
   `Tools-and-Scripts/` are at exactly this level.

3. **OPSEC awareness.** You don't burn your own operation. The
   pre-flight checklist habit is what they're looking for.

4. **Scope discipline.** Knowing when to stop looking. A six-finding
   dossier with clean methodology beats a sixty-finding dossier of
   junk every time.

5. **Reporting that translates to non-technical stakeholders.** A
   plant location that becomes a KML that becomes a one-slide
   visual for a board deck. The transformation from "raw OSINT
   finding" to "decision-relevant insight" is the highest-paid
   skill in this whole field.

## Where to keep training after today

**Books:**
- Michael Bazzell, *Open Source Intelligence Techniques* (current
  edition) — the canonical reference. Updated annually.
- Chris Kubecka, *Hack the World with OSINT* (2019) — best deep
  reference on Censys query language. Heavy ICS/SCADA use cases that
  pair directly with the kind of work you did today on Caterpillar.

**Communities and CTFs:**
- Trace Labs Search Party CTFs (recurring live OSINT competitions
  for missing persons — high-value, free, real-impact)
- OSINT Curious Project
- bellingcat.com/resources

**Formal training:**
- SANS SEC487 (OSINT)
- SANS FOR578 (Cyber Threat Intel) — if TI is your direction

**Paid practice:**
- HackerOne, Bugcrowd, Intigriti scopes — you get real targets and
  occasionally real money
